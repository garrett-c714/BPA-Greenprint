How They Work

The ground and air heating pump system is comprised of two large air pumps. One pump is placed 10-15 feet down into the Earth’s crust, while the other pump remains on the surface. The pump that is buried beneath the surface is in charge of heating the household, while the pump on the surface is capable of cooling one’s house. The core of the Earth puts off heat waves due to its extreme temperatures. These heat waves are dispersed throughout the layers of the Earth causing the temperatures beneath the surface to be consistently warm. Unlike the heat from the sun that wanes during the winter months, the inner-Earth heat is always present. By digging down into the crust, the pump is able to be placed at a level where it can collect the heat, and pump it into the house. When air conditioning is needed, the surface level pump collects warm air from its surroundings, and runs it through a cooling system before dispersing it through one’s home. By utilizing the natural temperature of the Earth, the ground and air heating pump system is able to drastically reduce the energy consumption and negative environmental effects.

Energy Efficiency

A standard AC unit has a COP coefficient of performance, of 3 while geothermal pumps have a COP of 4, meaning that geothermal heat pumps convert 1 KwH of energy into 4 KwH of useful heating, while the standard AC is only 1 KwH to 3 Kwh of useful heat. A standard AC unit has an EER energy efficiency ratio of 11, while the Geothermal Heating Pump has a rating of 19, meaning that a standard AC unit outputs 11 BTU with an input of one watt, while Geothermal Heating Pumps output 19 BTU with an input of one watt. The geothermal pumps are able to convert about 30% of the available resources into heat.

Potential Challenges

The system must meet certain depth and length regulations under the house, and it cannot intrude on other's property.

Scale, a hard white substance, builds up in pipes and is difficult to clear out. This can lower efficiency of the pumps, or in extreme cases halt the heating process entirely, if left unchecked. With efficient maintnance the buildup can be prevented.

General liability insurance will cover the pumps if they are installed incorrectly, but they typically aren't covered under a home-owners policy.

One must acquire a building permit before installation. Most permits are fairly easy to obtain.

Because of the way HVAC units are installed, it is much easier to install them in areas with softer ground.
