How They Work

Thermal panels are similar to their usual counterpart solar panels. They both harness energy from the sun, and transform it into electricity in order to power oneâ€™s home; however, unlike solar panels, thermal panels capture the thermal or heat energy from the sun, rather than the light energy. This can be very useful in places that the temperature remains warm throughout the entirety of the calendar year. While thermal panels are a great environmentally-friendly way to power a house, they are not usually able to create enough energy to power a household on their own, so usually homeowners decide to pair these panels with the aforementioned solar panels or another form of energy production. With the two types of panels working in unison, they are able to maximize the energy potential from the sun.

Benefits And Savings?

Energy Efficiency

Tax Credit Deduction

On average, thermal panels convert about 50% of the sun's energy into usable and clean electricity, compared to fossil fuels, that harness only 40% of the potential energy, wasting nearly 60% while harming the environment in the process. The government pays for 10% of the price of thermal panels through a tax credit.

Potential Challenges

The zoning laws vary by state, so it is important to check state laws before installation.

The Home Owners Associations cannot legally regulate the installation of thermal panels, unless major quantities are installed. Most HOAs don't have any regulations regarding thermal panels.

Thermal panels are still in the early stages of development. While they are currently effective at producing eco-friendly energy, engineers hope to increase their efficiency and develop them as PV panels.

Typically most home-owners insurances will cover thermal panels.

One must acquire a building permit before installation. Most permits are fairly easy to obtain.
